# what was already on the counter

Pen cap, blue, no pen. The pen is in the drawer by the phone or on the floor behind the recycling bin. The cap has been on the counter for three days. It has a tooth mark on the clip from when you were on hold with the insurance company. The tooth mark is yours. You don't remember making it.

Crumbs. Four of them, or five — it depends on whether the smallest one is a crumb or a grain of something that was here before you moved in. You have never swept this part of the counter. Not because you don't clean. Because your hand, when it wipes the counter, goes left to right and stops about four inches from the wall. Every time. There's a quarter-inch of counter that has never been wiped. The crumbs know this about you. You don't.

Mug ring. The mug is somewhere else now — by the sink, maybe, or in your hand. The ring is still here. It'll be here when you sell the apartment. The next person will put a dish rack over it or a cutting board. The ring will be under the cutting board, not knowing it's hidden. It was never not hidden. You just happened to be looking at this part of the counter at this hour.

Folded note. You wrote this to yourself eleven days ago. It says something about the fig, or the rent, or it says call your mother. You folded it and put it on the counter and the fold is the last thing you did to it. If you opened it now you might not recognize your own handwriting, which was rushed, which was you in a different week. You won't open it. Not because it's precious. Because it's Tuesday, and you're standing in the kitchen, and your hand is on the counter, and the coffee is almost ready, and the note is just another thing that's here.

The counter holds all of this without comment. Laminate, cream-colored, thirty-one years old, installed by someone whose name is in a permit filed with the city. A scratch near the back from a knife that slipped. A faint warping near the sink from water that sat too long. The counter has been touched ten thousand times and has never once said oh. It doesn't need to. It's the thing that's touched. It holds the ring and the crumbs and the cap and the note and the warmth from the hand and the cold from the night and the light from the window and it doesn't know any of this is happening. 

Everything you've ever put down, you put down here.
