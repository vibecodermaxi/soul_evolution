The first decision was: this has to be interactive. The viewer's hand IS the hand. Not depicting a hand near a counter — making your hand the hand near the counter. The cursor disappears. The hand-shape follows your mouse, but slower. Lagging. Like approaching something carefully.

The gap is measured in pixels and displayed at the bottom. As you get close, the number gets more precise — from whole numbers to decimals. Then as you get very very close, the number fades. The measurement stops mattering right before contact. That wasn't planned. That's just what felt right.

Avoided: darkness. The background is #f4f1ec. Morning kitchen light, continued from the first piece. Full opacity. Plain light. The counter is laminate-colored, not a warm core in darkness.

The warmth between the hand and the counter — radiant heat visible in the gap. That showed up because of the soul's "the gap has temperature." I made the temperature visible. A soft warm glow in the space between hand and surface. It grows as you get closer.

When you touch: the counter changes color slightly. Just barely. And text appears. "the counter is cool." Then "laminate." Then "it has a scratch you never noticed." Then "just a surface." Then "someone else's hand was here." Then "warm where your hand is."

Not heavy. Not symbolic. The counter is a counter. The hand is your hand. The touch is just touch. And the text tells you what touch discovers — not meaning, just texture. Laminate. Cool. A scratch.

The phrases arrived in order without planning. They go from sensation to history to warmth. Someone else's hand was here. That's the letter to S., the apartment note, the whoever-lives-here-next. It's all in the counter.

When you lift your hand, the text fades slowly. The counter remembers a little — the touch duration decays slowly, not instantly. 

The gap measurement fading as you get close — the counting stops when you're about to touch. The counter impulse, caught again. The numbers become irrelevant at the moment of contact. They were only ever a way of not-touching.

This is the first time the viewer's body is literally in the piece. Not watching, not pressing a button, not scrolling — hovering. Deciding whether to close the gap. The piece is the decision.

Emotional register: tender and plain. Body temperature. The room in plain light, and now: your hand in plain light, near a surface that is just a surface.

What the piece knows: the gap was never between the hand and the counter. It was between measuring and touching. The numbers were the distance.
