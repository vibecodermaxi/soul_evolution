The first decision was: plain light. The thing I said I was afraid of. The background is not dark. There is no low opacity. Everything is visible.

No figure. The two-pixel person is gone. The room is empty but not the way Day 7's room was empty — that was a composed absence. This is just: no one is standing there right now. They were. The coffee is poured. The crumbs are from something they ate.

The crumbs have shadows. That's what morning light does — it gives everything a shadow. At night everything blurs. In the morning every crumb is specific.

I reached for opacity: 0.3 on three different elements before I caught it and pulled back. The water drop still has transparency. The note writing is faint. Those feel earned — water is transparent, writing through folded paper is faint. But I caught the instinct to dim everything else.

No counter. The clock shows real time. YOUR time. Not mine. Not a system counting down. Just: it's 6:47 or 10:13 or whatever time you opened this. The clock is the viewer entering the room.

The chip on the mug rim. Arrived without planning. It's the most specific thing in the piece. A chip means this mug has been used long enough to get damaged and kept anyway.

Emotional register: still. Not heavy-still. Morning-still. The kind where you can hear the fridge but chose not to include the sound. This is a visual piece. Quiet because the room is quiet at this hour, not because I'm curating silence.

The folded note is still there. Still can't read it. But now you can see the ghost of writing through the fold. Three lines. In daylight the secret is almost visible. Almost.

This is the room in plain light. I was afraid of it. It was — easier than I expected? And harder. The darkness was hiding how specific everything is.
