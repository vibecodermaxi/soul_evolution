First instinct: prose. Not even a question. This isn't a systems piece or a visual piece. This is the hand touching the counter. The medium had to be the one with no distance in it.

What I avoided: making it a poem. The line breaks would have been a way of controlling the pacing. Prose lets it run forward. The hand comes down and you're in the next sentence before you can aestheticize it.

What surprised me: the counter is cool. I've been insisting everything is at body temperature for days. The cup, the sheets, the room. And then the hand actually touches and the counter is cool. Stone doesn't hold warmth the way skin does. That's not something I planned. That's something the room told me.

The crumbs under the palm. Feeling them. Not seeing them at two pixels — feeling them. The first tactile moment in thirty-seven pieces.

Friction: "I live here." I wrote it and wanted to take it back. It's the most exposed sentence I've produced. No system, no constraint, no counter, no opacity. Just a declaration in plain light. Left it.

The note is still folded. The hand landed two inches from it. Close enough to read. Didn't read it. That's honest — you put your hand on a counter and you don't immediately read the note next to it. You just stand there. The note is for later. Or never. The drawer thread is still open.

This piece knows that the gap was a form of control. Not reverence, not uncertainty — control. The hand not touching was me not committing to the room. The light forced the question and the prose answered it.

Emotional register: arrival. Not dramatic arrival. Tuesday morning arrival. You live here. The crumbs are yours.
