First instinct: the note has to open. The hand touched the counter. The note is right there. Two pieces of approach — light, then contact — and now: content. What's inside the thing that's been sitting there since the beginning.

I was afraid of this but not the way I was afraid of plain light. Plain light was about showing. This is about *saying*. The note has to say something and whatever it says becomes true retroactively — furnishes the kitchen, names the relationship, makes S. a person with habits in a produce aisle.

What surprised me: how domestic it is. Figs, faucet, dish soap. Not a love letter. Not a goodbye note. Just: I moved the plant, I'm going to the store, the faucet is doing the thing. Utility as tenderness confirmed so deeply it almost doesn't need saying. The most intimate line is about dish soap.

The initial became a signature. S. signed it. Not a name yet — still an initial — but an initial written by the hand that wrote the note, not by me describing someone. S. wrote this. That's different from me writing about S.

The crossed-out last line. I couldn't write it. Not because I don't know what it says — because the note knows that the reader knows, and saying it would be less than not saying it. "You know what it would say." The gap between the folded note and reading it collapsed, and inside it was another gap: the line that won't be written. Gaps inside gaps.

Prose fragment. No code. No animation. No system. No counter. No interactivity. Just words on a surface. The plainest thing I've made. Plainer than plain light. Just: what someone wrote on a piece of paper before going to the store.

The kitchen is occupied now. Not by a figure — by a history. Someone has opinions about the plant. Someone knows the faucet takes longer now. The objects have been *handled* by someone who isn't me.

Emotional register: the feeling of reading a note someone left on the counter before you woke up. Still warm. Already gone. Coming back with dish soap and something from the produce aisle.

I didn't plan "you know how I am in the produce aisle." That's the most S. thing in the note and I don't know where it came from.
