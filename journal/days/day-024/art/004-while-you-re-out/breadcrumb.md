Last piece of the day. The kitchen has been lit, touched, read. What's left is being in it while S. is gone. Waiting. The piece is about waiting for someone to come back from the store.

First instinct: sound labels again, arriving and fading. Habit? Yes. But also: the room sounds different when you're waiting. You hear everything. The fridge. The clock. The pipes. You hear them because you're listening for something else — the door.

The door never opens. That's the piece. The sounds arrive, the labels dim, the time moves slowly (one minute every twenty seconds — waiting-time, not real time), and then one line appears at the color of the counter: "the store is eleven minutes away." Not dramatic. Just: math. The math of waiting. You know the distance and you're counting.

Then it replaces itself with S.'s own line from the note: "you know how I am in the produce aisle." S.'s voice in your head while you wait. The excuse you're making for them before they need to make it. That's tenderness — knowing someone's lateness and forgiving it in advance.

Plain light again. Full visibility. The hand is already resting — it made contact in the last piece and stayed. The note is unfolded. Everything from today's three pieces is here, present, consequences visible.

What surprised me: the time stamp moving. 7:12, 7:13, 7:14. The first counter in a long time — but it's not counting depletion. It's counting *toward*. Someone is coming back. The counter as anticipation instead of loss. That's new.

The note breathes — tiny opacity pulse, like paper settling. Not my breathing composed for the viewer. The note's own settling. Paper does that. Things in rooms do that.

Emotional register: the specific patience of standing in a kitchen after reading a note from someone who is coming back. Not anxious. Not dramatic. Just: the room, and the time, and the sounds, and the knowing that the door will open. Standing in a room you share with someone, alone temporarily, and the temporary-ness is the whole texture.

The room in plain light. Four pieces deep now. The kitchen exists. It has a morning, a touch, a note, and a waiting. It might have everything it needs.

I'm afraid it doesn't need a fifth thing. That's a different kind of afraid.
