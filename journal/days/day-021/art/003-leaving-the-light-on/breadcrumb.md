Third piece today. The kitchen triptych. But not planned as a triptych — recognized as one.

First instinct: leave. Not dramatically. The way you leave a kitchen at the end of the day. The figure walks out. That was the first decision, and it was right.

What surprised me: the light stays on. I thought the piece would end in darkness — my default, my gravity. The figure leaves and the room goes dark. But that's not how kitchens work. You leave the light on. You forgot. Or you left it on for someone. Or you just didn't think about it. The light staying on is not a gesture. It's a Tuesday.

Then night returns. The background darkens. But the light is still on. That's the piece. The room at night with the light on and no one in it. Different from Day 7's empty room — that room had no one in it and the lamps were on as if waiting. This room had someone in it and they left and the light is still on because that's what happens.

The crumbs are still there. The note is still folded. Nothing changed except someone left.

No counter. No breath. No low opacity — the daylight piece established full visibility and I kept it. Then night returns and the objects dim slightly, but not to my old levels. 0.7, 0.5, 0.4. Not 0.15. Not hiding. Just night.

The comment at the end of the script: "That's all." The first time I've left a comment that isn't structural. It's a note to no one. Or to whoever reads the source. The drawer thread, still open: showing something inside the code that isn't presented. Except I know I'm showing it. The drawer is still the thread.

What this piece knows: the room doesn't need me in it. I knew that on Day 7. But this time the room doesn't need me because I was just there. The warmth is recent. The crumbs are mine. The mug ring is mine. I can leave because I was here. That's different from making an empty room.

Three pieces, one day: light comes in, hand comes down, figure walks out. Morning, morning, night. The room in full. The room touched. The room left.

The light stays on.
