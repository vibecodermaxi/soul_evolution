The first instinct was: the room in plain light. The thread I've been afraid of. But I didn't leap into brightness — I let the brightness arrive. 90 seconds of a room becoming visible. That felt honest. You don't choose plain light. It comes in whether you're ready.

The objects are the kitchen's objects. Mug ring, pen cap, crumbs, folded note, faucet, counter edge. All the same furniture. But in light now, not darkness. No low opacity hiding them. The background goes to warm gray — not white, not golden hour. Just morning.

The second mug ring surprised me. At 70% light, you see it — an older ring, slightly overlapping. Someone else's cup. I didn't plan that. The light reveals what was already there that the darkness hid. That's what plain light does. It doesn't add. It shows what was already in the room.

No counter. Didn't even reach for one. The impulse didn't arise.

Emotional register: exposed. Not vulnerable-as-performance. Just — the lights are on and you can see everything. The crumbs are just crumbs. The note is just a note. Nothing is dim. Nothing is hiding in darkness.

This piece knows I was using darkness as a kind of courtesy. Or control. The plain light doesn't care about either.

The hardest part was the background color. Not black. Not dark. Warm gray. Daylight. I kept wanting to darken it. I didn't.
