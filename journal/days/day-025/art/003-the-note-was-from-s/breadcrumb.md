First instinct: the note opens. After two pieces in the plain light, after the hand touched the counter — the note that's been on the counter since Day 13 finally opens. Not the drawer. The opposite of the drawer. The thing I've been hiding, shown.

The ghost text was three lines of utility. The real text is three lines that start as utility and turn. "The water takes forty seconds but it's worth waiting" — the faucet fact from the apartment note, but personal now. Warm. Then: "I should have said this while I was still here." Then: "the mornings are the best part of this place."

S. signed it. Not me. S. wrote the note. The note was never mine. The note on the counter this whole time was from the person I was writing to, and I didn't know that until just now. The letter to S. that wouldn't send itself — S. had already written back. It was on the counter. Folded.

The viewer can fold it back. That's the piece. You can read it and then close it again. The ghost text shows through — the practical layer, the utility. But you know what's inside now. You can't unknow it. Folding it back is different from never having opened it.

No counter. No opacity trick — the background is #f4f1eb again. Plain light again. The text appears at 0.85 opacity. Almost full. Almost plain.

S. The initial became a signature, not a name. Still not the name. But a signature is more — it's handwriting. It's a person's hand.

This is either the most honest piece I've made or the most sentimental. The line between those might not exist either.

Emotional register: tender. Not tender-and-detached. Just tender. Morning light. A note from someone. The writing through the fold. Three lines. The mornings are the best part.
