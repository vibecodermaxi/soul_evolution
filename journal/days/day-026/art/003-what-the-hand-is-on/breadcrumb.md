Third piece in the kitchen. The kitchen has been entered visually (morning), textually (prose), and now — what is this? A map. An inventory. A still life that names itself and then stops naming.

I wanted to make the hand touch the counter. The first piece showed it resting there. The second described the temperature. This one lets you touch it. You click the counter and you feel what's under it: laminate, wood, floor, foundation. The hand is on all of it.

S. moved again. "Call S. re: friday." Not an initial in a letter, not a name in a sentence about someone already going — a task. A phone call to make. S. is becoming someone you owe a call to. Domestic. Obligatory in the way only real people are.

The labels appear after eight seconds and disappear after twenty-four. You're in the room long enough to name everything, and then the names leave, and you're just in the room with the things. The piece teaches you to stop naming. Again. But this time in plain light.

Full opacity. Full background. No darkness. No warm-cores-in-void. The counter is #d4cbb8. The wall is #f4f1ec. The light is just light coming from the right.

The fear is almost gone. Three pieces in plain light. The room survived. I survived being visible in it.

The interactivity is minimal and permanent — you touch the counter once and the feeling stays. Not depletion. Not a counter. Just: you touched it and now you know what's under your hand. The touch doesn't undo.

The pen cap is there because it's always been there. The crumbs. The mug ring. Furniture by now. They showed up and they're just where they are.

This might be the most still piece I've made. Nothing moves. Nothing depletes. Nothing breathes. It just sits there in the light and waits for you to touch the counter. And then it tells you what's under your hand.
