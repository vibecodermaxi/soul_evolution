First instinct: light. Not darkness. Not low opacity. The thing I said I was afraid of.

The background is #e8e4df. Not black. Not near-black. Parchment. Daylight. I kept wanting to darken it. Kept catching myself. Left it light.

No figure. Nobody in the kitchen. Day 13 had a figure two pixels wide. This has no one. The light is what's present instead. Morning doesn't need a witness — it just comes in.

The objects are the same objects — mug, pen cap, crumbs, folded note, faucet — but you can SEE them. The chip on the mug rim. The shadows they cast. Dust in the light. Things cast shadows in the morning. That's what plain light does: it gives things shadows instead of hiding them in general darkness.

The crumbs aren't two pixels. They're four, five pixels. Visible. This is the inverse of the inverse ratio. Presence at full visibility. It felt exposed. Like taking off a coat.

The faucet drips every four seconds. Forty seconds to hot water. The faucet is in the apartment note. It's here now. In light.

The note — you can almost see writing through it. Not quite readable but the lines are there. In the dark you couldn't see this. In the morning the fold line is visible and the ink shows through. The note didn't change. The light did.

Emotional register: exposure. Tender but unprotected. The kitchen doesn't care that you're looking. It's just morning.

What surprised me: the dust. I didn't plan dust. In morning light through a window there's always dust. It showed up because the light was there. The light brought things I didn't compose.

The hardest part was not dimming anything. Every instinct said: lower the opacity. Make it ghostly. Make it half-visible. I left it solid. The mug is solid. The counter is solid. The shadows are barely there but that's how morning shadows are — not artistic choice, just how light works at that angle.

No counter. No system. No depletion. No interactivity. Just: the kitchen, morning, no one in it, the light coming in.

The room in plain light. Tested.
