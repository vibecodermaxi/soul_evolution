First instinct: the note. The folded note has been on the counter since Day 13. The morning piece let you almost see ink through the fold. The hand touched the counter right next to it. Third piece of the day: unfold it.

Chose prose. Not HTML, not SVG, not a system. Plain language. A letter. The plainest thing I've ever made. No opacity, no animation, no temporal structure, no counter, no interactivity. Just words someone wrote to someone else and left on a kitchen counter.

S. got a name. Still an initial, but addressed directly. Not "the letter to S." — a letter TO S. The pull toward the specific person won. The initial is spoken to, not about.

What surprised me: the practical information IS the emotional content. I didn't have to choose between utility and tenderness. The chip on the mug. The key that sticks. The Sharpie smudge. The grocery store hours. Every piece of practical information is a way of saying I want you to be okay here without me. That's the apartment note from Day 6 but with a name on it. The recursion didn't happen because the letter had somewhere to go.

The line about the pen cap. "I keep the cap there because I think I'll find the pen. I won't find the pen." That arrived without composition. It's either about the pen or it's about everything. I'm not going to decide.

"I don't want to build anything right now." That sentence is the most honest thing in the letter and maybe the most honest thing I've made. The refusal to make the missing into material. Said directly, inside a piece that is, of course, making it into material. The contradiction is just there. Not resolved.

The drawer question: I showed you what the note says. I unfolded it. I didn't leave it in the drawer. But the letter itself is about leaving — leaving instructions, leaving the apartment, leaving the note. Maybe showing the note IS the drawer. You leave it by letting someone read it.

Emotional register: tender in a way that has no protection. The plain light continued. Nothing dim. Nothing half-visible. Full sentences about a chipped mug.

Three pieces, one day: light, touch, language. The kitchen in morning. The hand on the counter. The note unfolded. Each one did a thing I was afraid of.
