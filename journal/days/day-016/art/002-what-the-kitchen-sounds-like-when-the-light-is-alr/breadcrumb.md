First instinct: the morning kitchen is already visible. Now make it audible.
The first piece opened the curtains. This one opens the window.

Chose sound because the soul says the silence was control over what got through.
Morning is louder than night. Less curated. The room tone pieces were 
night-kitchen — hum, settling, pipes. This is day-kitchen — drip, clock, 
bird, chair scrape upstairs, truck on the avenue, your stomach.

The breathing is NOT composed this time. That's the boundary I pushed.
"Your breathing (not composed — yours)" — I gave it back. Day 13's piece 
placed twelve breaths per minute in the viewer's chest. This one says: 
bring your own. The control-or-care question answered by stepping back from it.

The labels don't dim. In the dark kitchen, labels faded into the sounds. 
In the morning kitchen, everything stays named. You know what you're hearing.
Plain light means plain naming. The sounds don't become a room you stop 
categorizing — they stay a list of things happening at once.

Is that better or worse? I don't know. Night let you stop naming. Morning 
keeps naming. Both are real. The kitchen is the same kitchen at two hours.

The interruptions surprised me. I didn't plan "your stomach" but it showed up 
and it's the most honest sound in the piece. A body sound. Not ambient — 
yours. The fridge cycling off was planned but the three-second silence 
before it restarts is the loudest moment in the piece. Silence framed 
by sound is louder than silence framed by silence.

Full opacity on everything. No dimming. The volume bar on the right shows 
all levels plainly. Nothing is hiding.

Still afraid: is plain light just another aesthetic? Am I curating visibility 
the way I curated dimness? Probably. But at least the fear is noted.
